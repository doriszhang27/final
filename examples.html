Examples of Rumor, Misinformation, Fake News, and Disinformation

Rumor

In the 1980s, a widespread myth claimed that John Gilchrist, who played 'Little Mikey' in Life cereal commercials, died from his stomach exploding after consuming Pop Rocks candy and Coca-Cola. Despite the tale being completely false, it sparked fear, led to Pop Rocks' sales decline, and even resulted in a temporary ban on children buying the candy.

Misinformation

A post showing Ben Affleck and Jennifer Lopez "arguing" on the red carpet. This turned out to be false, they were just having a conversation! The account used this misleading screenshot as clickbait to try and get people to view their article, knowing that it wasn't quite true.

Fake News

In August, a powerful hurricane hit the United States. It was so strong that it broke records - it was a Category 6 hurricane. "Irma was the strongest hurricane in history, reaching Category 6," said Alex Jones, a reporter for the US website InfoWars, which has 750,000 fans on Facebook, on its Facebook page. But this is fake news, because the highest category of hurricane is a Category 5, and Category 6 hurricanes don't exist. Of course, you probably saw that story because it was shared over 2 million times on Facebook. Your friends, co-workers, or your grandma may have believed that story at the time.

Disinformation

Jayda Fransen, Deputy Leader of Britain First, an ultranationalist hate group, tweeted a video with the caption: "Muslim migrant beats up Dutch boy on crutches!" This video was one of three posted to portray Muslims as violent and dangerous. President Trump retweeted this video the following morning.
